11.1 Overview

Clustering is a fundamental task in unsupervised learning in machine learning research, where the goal is to discover inherent groupings in data without predefined labels. In this unit, we focus specifically on text clustering, which refers to the process of grouping documents such that documents within the same cluster are highly similar to each other, while being significantly different from documents in other clusters.

In general, clustering relies on a notion of similarity or dissimilarity between data objects. For text data, this requires first representing documents in a suitable feature space (e.g., bag-of-words, tf*idf, or embedding-based representations), after which a distance or similarity function can be applied. Unlike structured numerical data, text documents are inherently high-dimensional, sparse, and noisy. They often contain irrelevant terms, synonyms, and variations in language that may negatively affect clustering performance. As a result, text preprocessing is a critical step. Techniques such as tokenization, stopword removal, stemming/lemmatization, and feature weighting are essential to improve the quality of document representations and ensure that clusters reflect meaningful semantic themes rather than surface-level word overlap.

To address limitations of purely distance-based methods, model-based clustering approaches, such as those based on Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), provide a more principled alternative. LDA-based clustering assumes that documents are generated from a mixture of latent topics, where each topic is a probability distribution over words, a language model, which allows documents to be represented as mixtures of topics rather than being assigned to a single cluster, enabling more flexible and semantically meaningful grouping of text data.

Clustering has a wide range of real-world applications. For example, in recommender systems, clustering can be used to group users with similar preferences or behavioural profiles, enabling more effective personalized recommendations. It is also widely used in document organization, topic discovery, search result grouping, and exploratory data analysis.

In Week 11, the lecture begins with an introduction to clustering and discusses how distance functions can be defined for text documents. It then reviews classical clustering methods, including the sum-of-squares (square-error) criterion, and demonstrates cluster formation using the K-medoids algorithm. The lecture further introduces LDA-based clustering, implemented using the Python scikit-learn package, and discusses evaluation methods such as precision or cluster purity. Finally, it extends the discussion to LLM-era clustering. The week concludes with an introduction to recommender systems, including traditional collaborative filtering techniques and LLMs for news recommendation.
